# LinkedIn engagement playbook

## Overview

Use this skill to support engagement after a post goes live from a personal LinkedIn profile. It helps draft first comments, find adjacent posts to engage with, write useful comments, prepare connection DMs, and run a next-day reply sweep.

The goal is not artificial engagement. The goal is to create real conversation around the post by adding useful context, asking sharp questions, and bringing relevant people into the discussion without sounding spammy.

## When to Use

Use this when the user asks for:

- A first comment under their own LinkedIn post
- Comments on adjacent LinkedIn posts
- A LinkedIn engagement boost workflow after publishing
- DM copy to ask relevant connections for perspective
- A next-day reply sweep after an engagement push
- LinkedIn comments that sound casual, thoughtful, and non-corporate

Do not use this for:

- Company-page support replies that require a formal brand voice
- Mass DM campaigns or engagement pods
- Requests to manipulate metrics with fake or low-signal interactions
- Cold spam to people with no topical relevance

## Core Principles

- Add value before asking for anything.
- Ask for perspective, not likes, comments, or reposts.
- Avoid generic praise like “great post” or “love this.”
- Keep comments short enough to feel human.
- Prefer mechanisms, examples, tradeoffs, reframes, and questions.
- Do not send identical DMs in a rapid burst.
- Verify live actions before reporting them as completed.

## Voice

A strong personal LinkedIn reply should feel like a smart colleague texting in public: casual enough to be approachable, specific enough to be worth reading.

Recommended format:

- 1 sentence by default, usually 5-18 words for quick replies
- 2-3 short sentences only when the thread genuinely needs nuance
- One clear idea per comment
- Use natural capitalization, especially at the start of a sentence. Do not force all-lowercase as a humanization trick.
- Start with a small connector only when it sounds natural
- Keep the structure simple. Prefer direct agreement or emphasis over overbuilt contrast.

Connector examples:

- `Tbf,`
- `Fair.`
- `One nuance:`
- `The tricky part is...`
- `The missing layer is...`
- `The incentive problem is...`
- `I'd separate two things here:`
- `This gets harder when...`
- `Exactly, and...`
- `I'd push this further:`

Do not overuse one opener. The connector should sound relevant to the author’s point, not templated.

## Avoid

Avoid phrases and behaviors that make comments look automated or low-signal:

- `great point`
- `love this`
- `I agree`
- `game-changer`
- `paradigm shift`
- `in today's landscape`
- `at the intersection of...`
- `unlock the potential`
- `X is the new Y`
- `not just X, but Y`
- `this is X, not Y`
- `the hidden cost is X, not Y`
- hashtags
- engagement-bait
- overt link-dropping
- excessive emojis
- long paragraphs
- self-promotion unless directly relevant

Rule of thumb: if the comment could appear under any post, rewrite it.

## Alice-style quick reply corrections

For Alice's personal LinkedIn replies, do not make comments look artificially lowercase or over-structured. Use normal sentence-start capitalization and keep the core idea direct.

Good corrected patterns:

- `Exactly! the hidden cost is the spread indeed`
- `Yes! the real test is when chain abstraction disappears into default user flows.`
- `Trust imo. cheaper software makes proof and reliability the actual moat.`

Avoid turning simple agreement into `X, not Y` contrast when the second half is unnecessary.

## Post-Publication Engagement Workflow

Use this after a post goes out from the user's personal LinkedIn page.

1. **Open the live post**
   - Read the actual post body.
   - If it includes a video, carousel, or link, inspect the content or transcript when available.
   - Identify the post's core themes and the specific discussion it should trigger.

2. **Suggest or post a first comment**
   - Add a concrete discussion hook.
   - Good hooks include a question, poll-style choice, or specific prompt.
   - Do not recommend editing or reposting while the post is fresh unless there is a serious mistake.

3. **Find 8-12 adjacent LinkedIn posts to comment on**
   - Search around the live post's core themes.
   - Prefer posts with active comments and authors in adjacent audiences.
   - Prioritize people who are likely to care about the topic: founders, operators, marketers, product leaders, investors, builders, researchers, analysts, or community leaders depending on the topic.
   - Use the adjacent-account filter before commenting:
     - Topic fit: active post theme match, e.g. AI pricing, AI agents, SaaS/GTM, Web3 infrastructure, DeFi/payments, AI search/discoverability.
     - Audience fit: founders, PMM/GTM/product operators, AI/Web3 infra builders, VCs/advisors, analysts, data/API people, credible local/network contacts.
     - Conversation potential: recent post with enough comments/reactions to join, but not pure viral noise.
     - Relationship value: prioritize 1st/2nd degree and credible 3rd-degree accounts likely to respond or remember the user.
     - Brand safety: skip previous founders, ex-employers, founders the user worked for, spammy gurus, engagement bait, and accounts where the only possible reply is generic praise.
     - Comment specificity: only post if the comment can add a mechanism, reframe, prediction, or useful question.

4. **Draft mechanism/reframe/question-based comments**
   - Each comment should add something real: a mechanism, example, tradeoff, reframe, prediction, or question.
   - Avoid asking people to read, like, or comment on the user's post.
   - If linking the post is useful, do it sparingly and only when context makes it natural.

5. **Find 10-15 relevant first-degree connections for DMs**
   - Prioritize topical fit and likely reply intent.
   - Choose people who have a reason to care about the discussion.
   - Avoid previous employers, sensitive contacts, or people the user has explicitly excluded.

6. **Use DMs that ask for perspective, not engagement**
   - The DM should come from the user's personal account.
   - It should ask for the person's take, not for a like or comment.
   - Include the post link only as context.

7. **Throttle actions**
   - Suggested cadence: comment on up to 8 posts, DM 5 people, wait 2-3 hours, DM 5 more.
   - Do not send identical messages in rapid bursts.
   - Reply quickly to inbound comments while the post is fresh.

8. **Run a next-day reply sweep**
   - 18-36 hours later, review replies under the user's comments from the engagement burst.
   - Reply where there is a natural opening: a question, nuance, disagreement, or author response.
   - Skip low-signal, already-answered, or awkward threads.

9. **Verify before reporting**
   - Check the LinkedIn UI after posting comments or sending DMs.
   - Count verified actions separately from drafted or attempted actions.
   - Do not claim a comment, reply, or DM was posted unless it is visible or otherwise confirmed.

## First Comment Patterns

### Discussion question

Use when the post makes a claim and you want to invite opinions.

Template:

> curious how others are seeing this: is the bigger bottleneck right now [option A], [option B], or [option C]?

### Tradeoff prompt

Use when the topic has competing priorities.

Template:

> the tradeoff I keep coming back to is [X] vs [Y]. which one do you think matters more over the next 6-12 months?

### Example request

Use when the post would benefit from lived examples.

Template:

> would be interesting to hear concrete examples from people building this. where have you seen this work in practice, and where does it still break?

### Prediction hook

Use when the post points to a trend.

Template:

> my bet: the first visible shift will be [specific prediction], not [obvious prediction]. curious if others see it differently.

## Adjacent Comment Patterns

### Mechanism

> The mechanism that matters here is [specific system/incentive/cost]. once that changes, the whole category starts behaving differently.

### Reframe

> One nuance: this is less about [surface-level framing] and more about [deeper framing]. that changes who captures value.

### Tradeoff

> Fair. the hard part is that [benefit] usually comes with [cost]. most teams underestimate the second part until they hit scale.

### Question

> The question I'd ask is: what has to become true for this to move from interesting to default behavior?

### Future extrapolation

> This gets more obvious once [trigger/event/platform shift] happens. then [current behavior] starts looking like a temporary workaround.

## DM Templates

### Warm connection

> hey [Name], I just posted about [topic] and thought of you because of your work on [specific area]. curious how you're seeing this from your side: [specific question].
>
> sharing the post for context, but mostly interested in your take: [link]

### Operator / founder

> hey [Name], quick one. I posted a take on [topic], and I think your operator perspective would be sharper than most people's here.
>
> what do you think is the real bottleneck: [A] or [B]? [link]

### Investor / advisor

> hey [Name], I posted about [topic] and would be curious how this shows up in the companies you talk to.
>
> are teams actually prioritizing this yet, or is it still mostly narrative? [link]

### Peer / friend

> this made me think of our earlier conversation about [specific thing]. I posted a version of the idea here and would be curious if it matches what you're seeing too: [link]

## Live Action Rules

If using an automation/browser tool to post comments or send DMs:

1. Ask for explicit permission before posting or sending messages.
2. Use the user's logged-in browser/profile.
3. Draft first, then post only approved copy unless the user explicitly requested autonomous execution for that workflow.
4. Avoid duplicate comments or DMs.
5. Verify every posted item in the UI.
6. Report posted, drafted, skipped, and failed items separately.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Commenting too generically**
   - Bad: “Great insight, thanks for sharing.”
   - Better: add a mechanism, tradeoff, example, or question.

2. **Asking for engagement directly**
   - Bad: “Would love if you could comment on my post.”
   - Better: “Curious how you're seeing this from your side.”

3. **Over-linking the post**
   - Links are useful in DMs for context, but dropping the link into many public comments can look spammy.

4. **Ignoring cadence**
   - Rapid identical DMs can trigger platform limits or make the user look automated.

5. **Reporting unverified actions as done**
   - Always separate confirmed posted actions from drafts or attempts.

6. **Skipping the next-day sweep**
   - Many good conversations happen under the adjacent comments, not just under the user's original post.

## Verification Checklist

Before reporting completion:

- [ ] Live post was opened and read.
- [ ] First comment was drafted or posted.
- [ ] Adjacent post list was reviewed for topical fit.
- [ ] Comments are specific, non-generic, and not self-promotional.
- [ ] DM targets are first-degree connections or explicitly approved contacts.
- [ ] DMs ask for perspective, not engagement.
- [ ] Cadence/throttling was respected.
- [ ] Posted comments/DMs were verified in the LinkedIn UI.
- [ ] Next-day reply sweep was scheduled or completed when relevant.
